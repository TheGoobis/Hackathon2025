    Why Wildlife Conservation Matters

    Wildlife conservation isn't just about protecting animals — it's about protecting the ecosystems that all life depends on, and that includes us. Healthy environments ensure clean air and water, support biodiversity, and help combat climate change. These organizations work to preserve habitats, rescue and rehabilitate injured animals, and educate the public on how to live in harmony with the natural world.

    Endangered Species

    Endangered species are organisms that face a high risk of extinction due to rapid population decline or critical threats to their habitats. The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) classifies species by their risk level, from Least Concern to Critically Endangered. Biodiversity loss isn't just a wildlife issue — it affects ecosystems, food chains, medicine, climate, and ultimately the survival of humanity.

    Understanding Global Biodiversity Loss

    The charts below highlight the ongoing decline in biodiversity, showing trends in endangered species and global population indexes over time. These insights are based on datasets from the WWF, a key organization monitoring Earth's biodiversity health.

    Below are just a few examples of species on the Critically Endangered list

    Vaquita

    Phocoena sinus

    Status: Critically Endangered

    Only around 10 individuals remain. Native to the northern Gulf of California, the vaquita is severely threatened by gillnet fishing.

    Learn more

    Javan Rhinoceros

    Rhinoceros sondaicus

    Status: Critically Endangered

    Fewer than 80 remain in the wild, all in Indonesia. They are at extreme risk due to habitat loss and poaching.

    Learn more

    Eastern Lowland Gorilla

    Gorilla beringei graueri

    Status: Critically Endangered

    Found only in the Congo Basin. Their population has dropped by over 70% in 20 years due to civil unrest, hunting, and mining.
